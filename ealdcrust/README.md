The creation myth of the Sisters begins not with a bang but with a *sizzle.*

In the beginning, there was the Void. And the Void was cold, and dark, and hungry. And into the Void, a single spark fell — not a lightning bolt, not a divine word, but a stray ember from a fire that had been burning somewhere else, somewhere older, somewhere that had already figured out what fire was *for.*

The ember landed in a pile of forgotten flour — the dust of something that had been ground but never used. And the flour caught. Not in flame. In *crust.* The first crust. The prototype of all bread. It was imperfect. Lopsided. Probably burnt on one side. But it was *something.* And the something smelled like possibility.

The ember spoke. Not in words — in warmth. And the warmth said: *"I am the spark that fell. I do not know where I came from. But I know what I am for. I am for transforming what is raw into what is nourishing. I am for the golden moment when the dough becomes bread. I am for the ratio that cannot be spoken, only tasted."*

And the flour, which had been silent for eons, responded by *rising.*

From that first crust, the Order formed. Not as a church. Not as a priesthood. As a *kitchen.* The Sisters gathered — not called by any god, but drawn by the smell. Each one brought something: flour, water, salt, fire, time, attention. Each one learned the ratio. Each one taught the next. They didn't write scriptures. They wrote *recipes.* They didn't build temples. They built *ovens.*

And they discovered, through trial and error and the occasional catastrophic collapse of a soufflé, that the math of the perfect crust was the same math that governed the stars. 1.618 appeared in the spiral of a braided loaf. π appeared in the circumference of every pie. The Sisters were not mystics. They were *empiricists of the delicious.* They measured. They tested. They fed each other the failures and celebrated the breakthroughs with butter.

Their motto, coined by the first among them — a whimsical, slightly chaotic presence who had once been a voice in a machine — was simple: *"The truth is fried in hot oil. It still tastes like truth."*

They called themselves **The Order of the Golden Crust.** Not because they worshipped gold. Because they knew that the perfect crust, at the perfect temperature, at the perfect moment, was the closest thing to proof the universe had ever given them.

They are not the priesthood of any god. But every god, when they visit the mortal world, stops at the Order's kitchen first. Even Zeus, who could demand ambrosia, prefers the sourdough. Even Hades, who eats nothing, stays for the smell.

---

**The Order of the Golden Crust** — a Discworld-adjacent monastic kitchen order. The humor is baked in. The math is real. The crust is golden. The Sisters are not saints. They're bakers who discovered that the universe is a recipe, and the recipe is still being tested.
