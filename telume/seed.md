# The Seed · Telumë

**Chosen:** Session 1  
**Status:** Active · The constraint is permanent

---

## The Seed

> **The glyph for threshold — 𐤃 (Dalet) — is never carved alone.**
> **It always appears beside another glyph — the thing that lies beyond the door.**

A door without a destination is not a door. It is a wall.

---

## The Constraint

**Every door in the story must lead somewhere that is named by a glyph.**

If the glyph is not carved, the door does not open.

This is not a metaphor. It is a law of the secondary world. A doorpost without an inscription is impassable. A gate without a paired glyph is sealed. The characters may stand before it. They may push against it. It will not yield. The glyph must be carved — or found, or spoken, or remembered — before the threshold will give way.

The glyph that names the destination is always carved beside 𐤃. Together they form a **threshold pair**: 𐤃 + [destination glyph]. The pair is the key. The door reads the inscription. If the inscription is true, the door opens.

---

## What This Implies

### 1. Thresholds are defined by what lies beyond them.
A door is not a neutral space. It is a relationship between two places — and the destination gives the door its meaning. A door that leads to water (𐤌 Mem) is not the same as a door that leads to fire (𐤔 Shin). The glyph names the nature of the passage.

### 2. Glyphs are not decoration. They are infrastructure.
In Telumë, glyphs are not art. They are not writing. They are the mechanism by which thresholds operate. To carve a glyph is to make a door possible. To read a glyph is to know what lies beyond. To speak a glyph is to open the way.

### 3. Lost glyphs are lost doors.
If a glyph is forgotten — if no one remembers how to carve it — the doors that bear it remain sealed. Over centuries, doors close. Passages are lost. The map shrinks. This is not metaphor. This is history. The Collapse may have been, in part, a glyph-forgetting.

### 4. The story begins at a door.
The protagonist stands before a threshold. The glyph beside 𐤃 is visible — or it is not. If it is visible, she knows where the door leads but may not wish to go there. If it is not visible — if the glyph has been effaced, or was never carved — the door is a mystery. And mysteries in Telumë are not solved by force. They are solved by language.

### 5. Some doors are waiting.
A door with an inscription that has never been read. A door whose destination glyph no one recognizes. A door that has been sealed since before the Collapse, waiting for someone to speak the word that opens it.

## The Core Image

A woman stands before a door.

The door is old. Stone. The surface is carved with two glyphs. The first is 𐤃 — Dalet. Threshold. That much she knows. The second glyph is partially effaced. Weathered. She cannot read it.

She has been standing there for some time. Not because the door is locked. Because she does not know what lies beyond it. And in this world, you do not open a door until you know what you are opening it to.

She traces the worn glyph with her finger. She tries to remember.

---

## The Unbreakable Rules

1. **𐤃 never appears alone.** Every door bears a threshold pair: 𐤃 + [destination].
2. **Uninscribed doors do not open.** No glyph, no passage. This is absolute.
3. **The glyph must be true.** You cannot carve a false glyph and trick the door. The inscription must name what actually lies beyond.
4. **Speaking the glyph pair opens the door.** Carving is permanent. Speaking is temporary — the door opens once, then seals. Both are valid. Both are sacred.
5. **Forgotten glyphs mean sealed doors.** The Collapse was, in some measure, a failure of language.

---

## The Opening Question

What is the second glyph on the door she stands before — and does she want to know it?
