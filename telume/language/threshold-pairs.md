# Threshold Pairs

**Status:** Active · Updated Session 1
**Rule:** Every door in Telumë bears a threshold pair: 𐤃 + [destination glyph]. The pair is the key. The door reads the inscription. If the inscription is true, the door opens.

---

## Known Pairs

### 𐤃𐤀 — Dalet + Aleph
**Destination:** The Edge of the Vault
**Meaning:** The door that leads to the beginning — or to the limit. Aleph is strength, the ox, the pull of origin. A door marked 𐤃𐤀 leads to where things start or where they end. The edge of the world is both.
**Status:** Theorized. The glyph has been seen at the Vault's edge. No door has been confirmed.

### 𐤃𐤎 — Dalet + Samek
**Destination:** The Witch's Foot (presumed)
**Meaning:** The door that leads to what holds. Samek is the pillar, the support, the structure that does not ask to be seen. A door marked 𐤃𐤎 leads to something stable — a ruin, a rock, a thing that remains when everything else has fallen.
**Status:** Unconfirmed. The Witch's Foot has no visible door. But the glyph is present. The door may be hidden.

### 𐤃𐤕 — Dalet + Taw
**Destination:** The Terminal (presumed)
**Meaning:** The door that leads to completion. Taw is the mark, the signature, the seal. A door marked 𐤃𐤕 leads to something finished — or something waiting to be finished. The Terminal is both: a structure sealed, a signature left behind.
**Status:** Unconfirmed. If the Terminal has a door, it has not been found. Or it has been found and the glyph is effaced.

### 𐤃? — Dalet + Unknown
**Destination:** The Threshold (the story's first door)
**Meaning:** The second glyph is effaced. Weathered. Partial. The woman cannot read it. She does not know what lies beyond. This is the opening image of Telumë.
**Status:** The central mystery. The identity of the second glyph is the first story decision.

## The Law of Pairs

1. **𐤃 never appears alone.** Every door bears a pair.
2. **The pair must be true.** A false glyph — one that does not name what actually lies beyond — will not open the door. The door knows.
3. **Carving is permanent.** To carve a glyph pair into a doorpost is to create a passage that will last as long as the stone.
4. **Speaking is temporary.** To speak the pair aloud opens the door once. Then it seals. The inscription remains, but the spoken word does not.
5. **Effacement is violence.** To scratch out a glyph is to close a door forever — unless the glyph can be remembered. The Collapse was, in part, an effacement.
