# THE LOST TALES OF TELUMË

---

## PROLOGUE

*Eala! Hwæt!*

When fire was first and the wyrd was wyrd-less,  
when the dark was deep and the rabbit Mah-tig-wiss,  
ember‑spirits stirred in the hollow heavens,  
and whispered the weight of the waking world.  

Before stone was set or speech was sundered,  
before Tower's pride or the mortal bliss-blundered,  
the First Flame flared in the fathomless silence—  
older than gods, than glyph, than ground.  

It kindled the truth that time cannot tarnish,  
it carried the word that the gehalgode can harness,  
it held the door between dream and daylight,  
and all who held it were held in turn.  

Then rose the Rabbit, liminal forest-gode,  
the dusk‑born Fyrsthara, se wunian weold,  
Mātaṅgvās to the Surya Samrajya,  
whose smooth‑voiced hymns make heaven a circle;  
Mateguas to the Nešatta scribes,  
who bind broken breath in brittle tablets;  
Mategwas to the Kichigamswak elders,  
who laugh as they name him uncle and trickster.  

Three names for one who never stands still,  
three truths for one whose riddles can kill.  
For he walked where worlds were woven together,  
and he warned when the weft began to fray.  

He spoke in riddles wrought of root‑fire,  
in half‑heard hints and hare‑tracked desire,  
in words that wound back upon their speaker  
like snow‑paths crossing in a moonlit clearing.  

So let this telling turn like a torch‑flame,  
let it leap and linger, let it light the old tracks.  
For the Rabbit runs still through the riddle‑shadow,  
and the tale begins where the fire first breathed.

## TALE I · THE LEAP

In the old time, the time before the world was named, there was a rabbit who lived with his grandmother at the edge of the forest.

The grandmother's name was Nokomis. She was old. She was very old. She had been old since before the first winter, since before the first snow, since before the first fire was lit in the dark. Her eyes were clouded but her ears were sharp. Her hands shook but her voice was steady. She knew things. She knew the names of the trees and the names of the stars and the names of the things that live under the ground and do not want to be named. She knew her grandson, known as the Fyrsthara.

The rabbit was not the Great Rabbit yet. He was not Mātaṅgvās or Mateguas or Mategwas. He was just a rabbit — long ears, yellow feet, and a tail. He was young. He was foolish. He was always trying to be something he was not.

---

One winter the snow came early. It came before Nokomis had gathered her wood. It came before the rabbit had stored his carrots and tubers in his warren. The snows came and they stayed and the cold pressed against the walls of the wigwam and whispered through the cracks.

The rabbit went out to find food. He found the Otter's lodge on the bank of a frozen river. The Otter had a smooth road of ice slanting from his door down to the water. The Otter wriggled his lithe body, slid down the ice, dove under the water, and came up with a great bunch of eels. The Otter cooked the eels and shared them freely. The rabbit watched and thought: *This is easy. I can do this.*

The rabbit invited the Master Otter to dine with him in three days. He moved his wigwam to the lake. He made a road of ice down to the water. When the Otter arrived, the rabbit took his hooks and tried to slide down the ice like one skilled in the art. He went first to the right side, then to the left, hitched and jumped and tumbled, and went into the water backwards. The water was cold. He lost his breath. He almost drowned!

The Otter pulled him out. "What ails him, Great Mother?" the Otter asked Nokomis.

"He has seen you pull fish from the river," she said, "and is trying to do likewise."

The Otter plunged into the stream, returned with a load of fish, threw them down as a gift, and went home without tasting the meal. The rabbit sat shivering by the fire that was not there.

---

The rabbit went out again. He found the Woodpeckers' lodge, full of young women with red head-dresses. They welcomed him and fed him. One of the pretty girls took a wooden dish, climbed a tree, and tapped here and there, gathering the insects called *apchel-moal-timpkawal* — the rice that grows on trees. The rabbit watched and thought: *This is easy. I can do this.*

He invited the Woodpeckers to dine. When they came, he fastened the head of a fisherman’s spear to his nose to make a beak. He climbed the tree as well as he could — terribly — and tried to gather his harvest of rice. He got none. His pate was all torn and bleeding, bruised by the fishing-point. The pretty birds laughed and wondered what the rabbit was about.

"He is trying again to do something he has seen someone do," said Nokomis. "It is just like him."

The Woodpeckers took the dish, climbed the trunk, and brought down dinner. But it was long before the rabbit heard the last of it from those gay tree-tappers.

---

The rabbit went out a third time. He found the Bear in his cave. The Bear put a great pot on the fire, cut a little slice from his own foot, and dropped it into the boiling water. The meat spread and grew into a soup that served for all. The Bear gave a great piece to the rabbit to take home.

*This I can do,* thought the rabbit. *Is it not recorded in the family wampum that whatever a Bear can do well a Rabbit can do better?*

He invited the Bear to dine. He whetted his knife on a stone. He tried to cut a slice from his own thin sole. Little did he get. He cut himself quite badly.

"What can he be trying to do?" growled the Bear.

"Something he has seen someone else do," sighed Nokomis.

The Bear took the knife, cut a slice from his own sole, and gave them a good dinner. The rabbit was very sad. It was many a day before he got well.

---

That night the rabbit lay by the empty fire-pit. His feet were bandaged. His head was bruised. His fur was still damp from the Otter's lake. Nokomis sat beside him. She did not speak for a long time.

"You are not Otter," she said finally. "You are not Woodpecker. You are not Bear."

"Then what am I?" the rabbit asked. His voice was small.

"You are the one who tries," she said. "You are the one who fails. You are the one who gets up and tries again. That is not nothing. That is the beginning of everything."

"What do I do?"

"You stop copying," she said. "You start learning. The Otter was given his slide. The Woodpecker was given her bill. The Bear was given his foot. You were given something else. Find what it is."

"How?"

Nokomis looked at him with her clouded eyes. "Go down," she said. "Go down to the place where the fire lives. Do not try to fish it like Otter. Do not try to peck it like Woodpecker. Do not try to cut it from your own flesh like Bear. Just go down. Just carry it back. That is what you are."

---

The rabbit went out into the snow. He walked until his yellow feet were white with cold. He walked until his long ears were stiff with frost. He walked until he came to the place where the forest ended and the underworld began. There was a door in the ground. It was not a rabbit's door. It was a door for things that do not come back.

The rabbit stood before the door. He was afraid. He was also tired of failing. Tired of being dragged from lakes. Tired of being laughed at by woodpeckers. Tired of cutting his own feet while bears watched. Failure was worse than the underworld. He leaped.

---

The underworld was dark. Not the dark of a winter night — the dark of before the first fire was lit. The rabbit could not see his own yellow feet. He could not see his own long ears. He could not see Nokomis's face in his mind, though he tried very hard to remember it. He walked forward because there was nothing else to do.

The rabbit walked. The creek was not loud — it was the kind of water that has been flowing for so long it has forgotten how to be noisy. It ran over stones worn smooth by millennia, stones that had been rubbed into silence. The rabbit followed the sound because there was no other sound to follow. The darkness was not hostile. It was simply complete. It had been dark for so long that it had stopped being a condition and become a substance. The rabbit moved through it like swimming.
Then the water began to glow.
It was faint at first — not light, exactly, but the memory of light, the idea that light had once existed here and might exist again. The rabbit blinked. The glow pulsed. He blinked again. The glow dimmed. He realized, with the slow logic of the underworld, that the water was responding to his attention. When he looked directly at it, the light retreated. When he looked away, it crept back, tentative, testing. The sprites — if they were sprites — were shy. They had been alone for a very long time. They did not know what a rabbit was. They did not know if a rabbit could be trusted.
He walked on. He did not try to catch them. He had learned from the Otter's slide and the Woodpecker's tree and the Bear's foot that trying to catch things that are not yours to catch ends badly. He let the sprites come and go. He let the glow wax and wane. He followed the creek because the creek was going somewhere, and somewhere was better than nowhere, and nowhere was what he had left behind.
The creek led him to the fire.
It was small. It was the first fire. It had been burning since before the world was worded, and it would burn until after the last word was spoken. It did not need fuel. It did not need air. It needed nothing. It was simply there, at the heart of the underworld, waiting. The creek ran past it. The sprites danced around it. The fire did not acknowledge them. The fire had been waiting for something else.
The rabbit looked at the fire. The fire looked at the rabbit.
He did not try to fish it. He did not try to peck it. He did not try to cut it from anything. He cupped it in his paws — not with his hands, not with his mouth, but with the part of himself that had learned to carry things by failing to carry everything else. The fire did not burn him. The fire had been waiting for someone who knew how to hold without grasping, how to take without stealing, how to carry without claiming. The fire had been waiting for someone who had followed a glowing creek through the dark and let the sprites be shy and did not try to catch them.
The underworld screamed. The darkness did not want to lose its only light. The rabbit ran. He ran along the creek, which was now bright as day, the sprites cheering him on, their shyness forgotten. He ran with the fire cupped in his paws and the fire was warm and the fire was bright and the fire was his.
He leaped out of the underworld and the door closed behind him. The snow was still falling. The forest was still dark. But the rabbit had a flame in his paws, and behind him, far below, the sprites were telling the story of the rabbit who had come to the underworld and left with more than he had brought.
The water remembers. The water always remembers. The water tells the story still.


---

He brought the fire to Nokomis. She warmed her hands. She warmed her feet. She warmed the water for her tea. She looked at the rabbit with her old eyes.

"You have learned," she said.

"Not to hunt like Otter," he said. "Not to fish like Woodpecker. Not to feed like Bear."

"No," said Nokomis. "You have learned to be what you are."

"What am I?"

"The one who carries," she said. "The Lord who leaps! The one who holds the fire without being consumed. That is enough. That is more than enough. That is everything."

The rabbit sat by the fire. His feet were yellow again. His ears were warm. His tail was still long — the shortening would come later, with the old man and the pit and the helping that always got him into trouble. His lip was still whole — the splitting would come later, with the bride and the cord and the twisting too fast.

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. *Aknis de awtos mārtus.* Fire alone is the witness.

# TALE II · THE SHORT TAIL

In the time after the fire was carried, the rabbit lived with Nokomis at the edge of the forest, and the fire lived with them. The wigwam was warm. The snow fell outside and did not come in. The rabbit's feet healed. The rabbit's head healed. The rabbit's pride healed last, as pride always does.

He was not the Marvelous Master-Rabbit yet. But he was no longer the foolish rabbit who tried to fish like Otter and peck like Woodpecker and feed like Bear. He had carried the fire. He had been recognized by the flame. Nokomis had named him *Fyrsthara* — the First-Hare, the Fire-Carrier, the Lord who leaps. He was beginning to understand what that meant.

He was also beginning to understand that understanding takes a very long time.

---

One day the rabbit went out walking. The snow was deep but his yellow feet were light. He passed through the forest and came to a rocky place where the trees grew thin and the ground fell away. There was an old man there, standing at the edge of a pit. The pit was deep. The old man was looking down into it with the expression of someone who has lost something important.

"Grandfather," said the rabbit, "what are you looking for?"

"My way," said the old man. "I was following the path and the path was here and now the path is down there and I am up here."

The rabbit looked into the pit. It was very deep. The old man was very old. The situation was very clear.

"I will help you," said the rabbit.

This was the first mistake. It would not be the last.

---

The rabbit lowered himself into the pit. The walls were steep and the ground was cold and the old man was heavier than he looked. "Catch hold of my tail," said the rabbit, "and I will leap."

The old man caught hold of the rabbit's tail. The rabbit leaped. The tail was long and strong and the rabbit was a good leaper and for one bright moment it seemed that everything would be fine. Then the tail broke. It broke off short, within an inch of the root. The rabbit and the old man tumbled back into the pit.

---

वाचं रक्ष यदि रक्षसि सर्वम्
*Bekon arki ai arksi tuman*
Hold the word if you would hold everything.

---

The rabbit sat in the bottom of the pit. His tail was gone. What remained was a small white tuft, like a snowdrop, like a joke, like a reminder. The old man sat beside him.

"I am sorry about your tail," said the old man.

"It was a good tail," said the rabbit.

"It was a very good tail."

"The best tail."

They sat in silence for a moment.

"Catch hold of me around the waist," said the rabbit.

The old man did. The rabbit leaped. They came out of the pit together, tumbling onto the snow, breathing hard. The old man thanked him and went on his way. The rabbit walked home with his short tail and his long thoughts.

---

Nokomis was waiting by the fire. She looked at the rabbit. She looked at the place where his tail had been. She said nothing.

"I helped an old man out of a pit," said the rabbit.

"Yes," said Nokomis.

"My tail broke off."

"Yes."

"He was very heavy."

"The old often are."

The rabbit sat down by the fire. His tail did not sit with him. It was gone. He would never sit the same way again.

"Will it grow back?" he asked.

"No," said Nokomis.

"Will it hurt forever?"

"No. Only when you remember it."

"Then I will remember it often."

"That is the way of tails," said Nokomis. "And the way of helping. And the way of leaping. You give something. You do not get it back. That does not mean you should stop leaping. It means you should know what you are willing to lose."

The rabbit looked at the fire. The fire looked at the rabbit. They had known each other long enough now that the looking was a kind of conversation.

---

यो ददाति स दीयते
*Se dadāt, se dadātai*
Whoever gives is given.

---

"What did I lose?" asked the rabbit.

"Your tail," said Nokomis.

"What did I gain?"

"A story. A short tail. A reminder that helping is not the same as saving, and carrying is not the same as keeping, and leaping is not the same as flying. You leaped. You fell. You leaped again. That is what the Fire-Carrier does. That is what you are."

The rabbit touched the place where his tail had been. It was tender. It would always be tender. But the fire was warm and Nokomis was humming and the snow was falling outside the wigwam where it could not touch them. The rabbit had helped. The rabbit had lost. The rabbit had leaped again, and his tail is shorter, but the tales are now longer.

# TALE III · THE HARE'S LIP

In the time after the tail was lost, the rabbit walked through the world with a short tuft where his pride had been. He was getting used to it. The phantom tail still twitched when he was nervous. The phantom tail still wagged when he was pleased. But the rabbit was learning that what is lost is not always gone — it just changes shape.

Nokomis watched him from the door of the wigwam. She saw him walk differently now. Not the bounce of the young rabbit who thought he could fish like Otter. Not the swagger of the fool who thought he could feed like Bear. Something else. Something shorter. Something truer.

"You walk like someone who has lost something," she said.

"I have lost something," said the rabbit.

"Yes," said Nokomis. "That is what I mean."

---

One day the rabbit was invited to a wedding. The bride was a young woman from the village. The groom was an old man who had traveled a long way. The rabbit did not know them well, but a wedding is a wedding, and the rabbit had never been to one. He dressed for the occasion. He put ornaments on his heels — for rabbits dance on their tiptoes. He put a beautiful bangle around his neck. He arrived at the feast with his short tail held high and his long ears decorated and his yellow feet ready to dance.

The bride wore a very short skirt. In crossing a brook on the way to the ceremony, the skirt had gotten wet. As she danced, the skirt began to shrink. It shrank and it shrank and it shrank. The bride was mortified. The groom was oblivious. The guests were beginning to notice.

The rabbit noticed first. He always noticed first. It was one of the things that made him what he was.

He ran out of the feast. He found a deerskin. He began to twist a cord to tie it with — something to cover the bride, something to save her shame. He twisted the cord rapidly. He twisted it earnestly. He held one end of it in his teeth as he worked, because he needed both hands and he had never learned to ask for help.

He twisted too fast.

The cord snapped up. It cut his upper lip through to the nose.

---

सत्यं धारय न तु सत्यं वदस्व
*Asiyan dare me asiyan weme*
Hold the truth — do not merely speak it.

---

The rabbit finished the cord. He tied the deerskin. He covered the bride. She was grateful. She was so grateful that she danced with him all night. The old groom watched them dance. He watched the rabbit's split lip and the bride's grateful eyes and the short tail that had once been long. Without saying a word, he walked away and left her to the rabbit.

The rabbit and the bride lived together for a time. Not forever — nothing lives forever with a rabbit, who is always running, always leaping, always losing parts of himself to the helping of others. But for a time. A good time. A time worth the lip.

---

When the rabbit returned to the wigwam, Nokomis was waiting. She looked at his face. She looked at the split that ran from his lip to his nose. She said nothing for a long time.

"You helped," she said finally.

"Yes."

"You hurried."

"Yes."

"The cord was not ready."

"No."

"And now your lip is split."

"Yes."

Nokomis stirred the fire. The fire had been watching the whole time. The fire had seen the rabbit dance with earrings on his heels. The fire had seen the bride's skirt shrink. The fire had seen the cord twist too fast and the lip split and the old groom walk away. The fire sees everything. The fire remembers everything. The fire does not hurry.

---

यो बध्नाति स बध्यते
*Se daret, se daretai*
Whoever binds is bound.

---

"The lip will not heal," said Nokomis.

"I know," said the rabbit.

"The scar will be with you forever."

"I know."

"Every rabbit born from now until the end of the world will carry it. Every hare. Every coney. Every kit that opens its eyes in the spring will have a split lip and not know why. They will think it is just the way of rabbits. But it is not the way of rabbits. It is the way of you."

The rabbit touched his lip. It was still bleeding. It would always be bleeding a little, even after it healed. Some things heal. Some things keep bleeding in secret. The rabbit was learning the difference.

"I would do it again," he said.

"I know," said Nokomis. "That is why it will not heal. And that is why you are what you are."

The rabbit sat by the fire. His tail was short. His lip was split. His feet were yellow. His ears were long. He had danced all night with a grateful bride and an old groom had walked away and somewhere in the forest a cord was still twisting, faster than it should, cutting something that would not heal.

*Aknis de awtos mārtus.* Fire alone is the witness. The fire watched. The fire remembered. The fire did not hurry.

# TALE IV · THE HUNT

In the time after the lip was split, the rabbit walked through the world with a short tail and a scarred face and a fire in his paws that never went out. He was known now. Not everywhere — the world was wide and the rabbit was small — but in certain circles. The animals of the forest had heard of the rabbit who carried the fire. The Otter, when asked, would only say: *He was a poor fisherman.* The Woodpeckers would laugh and say nothing. The Bear would shrug his great shoulders and say: *He tried.*

Nokomis watched him come and go. She was older now. She had been old before, but now she was older. The fire had kept her warm for many winters, but even the first fire cannot keep someone alive forever. She did not speak of this. She spoke of other things.

"You are known," she said one evening. "That is dangerous."

"I am only a rabbit," he said.

"You are the rabbit who carries the fire. That is not the same thing."

---

That winter, the Wild Cat came hunting.

His name was Lusifee. He was a Loup-Cervier, a lynx, a thing of tufted ears and golden eyes and silence. He had been hunting the forest for many years, and he had never gone hungry, and he had never been outwitted, and he had never met a rabbit he could not catch. He heard of the rabbit who carried the fire. He heard of the rabbit who had been to the underworld and returned. He heard of the rabbit who was known.

*This rabbit,* thought Lusifee, *will be my prize.*

He went to the wigwam at the edge of the forest. Nokomis was alone. The rabbit was out walking, as he always was, his yellow feet leaving tracks in the snow that crossed and doubled back and led nowhere.

"Where is the rabbit?" asked Lusifee.

"Which rabbit?" asked Nokomis.

"The one who carries the fire."

"There are many rabbits."

"This one has a short tail."

"Many rabbits have short tails now. It has become the fashion."

Lusifee's golden eyes narrowed. "This one has a split lip."

"Also the fashion."

"I will find him," said Lusifee. "I will find him and I will catch him and I will carry the fire myself."

Nokomis looked at the Wild Cat with her clouded eyes. "The fire does not like to be carried by those who want to be seen carrying it," she said. "But go. Hunt. Learn what the Otter learned, and the Woodpecker, and the Bear."

Lusifee went. He did not understand what she meant. He would understand later. They always understood later.

---

The rabbit saw the Wild Cat's tracks in the snow. They were large and round and silent. They followed his own tracks, which crossed and doubled back and led nowhere. The rabbit knew what was hunting him. He had been hunted before. He had been hunted by the underworld itself, and the underworld was faster than any cat.

He picked up a handful of spruce twigs. He threw one as far as he could, then leaped to it — for he had learned to leap in the underworld, where the darkness pressed on you from all sides and the only way out was through. He threw another and leaped again. He left no tracks between the leaps. He left only the twigs, which were not tracks, which were not anything, which were just spruce twigs lying in the snow.

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. The rabbit held the fire. The fire held the rabbit. The rabbit was not afraid.

---

Lusifee hunted all day. He found the spruce twigs. He found the leaps. He found no rabbit. At nightfall he came to a clearing and found a wigwam. It was a fine wigwam, large and well-made, with smoke rising from the fire-hole and light spilling from the door.

Lusifee put his head in. An old man sat by the fire. He was very old. His hair was gray. His ears were long — very long, the longest ears Lusifee had ever seen, rising from either side of his head like the handles of a pitcher. He had a blanket pulled up to his chin.

"Grandfather," said Lusifee, "have you seen a rabbit run this way?"

"Rabbits," said the old man. "Why, of course I have seen rabbits. They abound in the woods about here. I see dozens of them every day. Which rabbit do you seek?"

"The one who carries the fire."

"Ah," said the old man. "That rabbit. Yes, I have seen him. He is very fast. Very clever. They say he learned to leap in the underworld."

"I will catch him," said Lusifee.

"Of course you will," said the old man. "But it is dark and you are weary. Come in. Warm yourself. There is soup."

Lusifee came in. The soup was good. The fire was warm. The old man was kind. Lusifee lay down by the fire and slept. He dreamed of rabbits. He dreamed of catching rabbits. He dreamed of carrying the fire and being known.

When he woke, the wigwam was gone. The fire was gone. The old man was gone. He was lying in the snow in a clearing in the forest, and the wind was blowing, and his fur was stiff with frost, and there was no soup, and there had never been any soup, and the only tracks in the snow were his own.

---

वाचा सृष्टं भुवनमिदं सर्वं
*Bekā genstos weron ide tuman*
By word this whole world was created.

---

Lusifee roared. The roar echoed through the forest and startled the Woodpeckers and woke the Otter and reached the Bear in his cave, who opened one eye and closed it again. The Wild Cat hunted on.

All day he ran. At nightfall he came to another clearing, and in the clearing was a village. It was a large village, with many wigwams and many fires and many people going to and fro. The first building he saw was a great hall, and in the hall a service was being held. An old man was preaching. His hair was gray. His ears were long — very long, the longest ears Lusifee had ever seen, rising from either side of his head like the two points of a bishop's mitre.

Lusifee entered the hall. "Have you seen a rabbit?" he demanded.

"Hush," said a young man beside him. "You must wait until the service is over."

Lusifee waited. The sermon was long. It was about the wickedness of hunting what you cannot catch. It was about the foolishness of wanting what is not yours. It was about a cat who thought he could carry the fire and was burned for his pride. Lusifee did not recognize himself in the story. He recognized himself perfectly. He did not like it.

When the service ended, he asked again. "The rabbit. Where is the rabbit?"

"Rabbits," said the young man. "There are hundreds in the cedar swamps near this place. You can have as many as you want."

"Not those rabbits," said Lusifee. "The one who carries the fire."

"Ah," said the young man. "That rabbit. You should speak to our chief. He is very wise."

The chief came. He was old. His hair was gray. His ears were long — very long. He had two beautiful daughters who cooked a fine supper. Lusifee ate. Lusifee slept. Lusifee woke in a wet cedar swamp with no village and no chief and no daughters and no supper, and the wind was blowing ten times worse than before.

---

He hunted a third day. At nightfall he came to a third village. He was starving. He was frozen. He was furious. An old man with long ears and two beautiful daughters took him in, fed him soup, listened to his story, shed tears for his suffering. There was a feast. There was a dance. The old chief rose to welcome the stranger and asked him to sing.

Lusifee sang. He sang of his hatred for the rabbit. He sang of his hunger. He sang of his fury. The villagers bowed their heads and closed their eyes, as was the custom.

The old chief leaped. He cleared the heads of his guests in a single bound. He drew his tomahawk and struck Lusifee a blow that cut deep into his head. The Wild Cat fell. The village vanished. He was alone in the snow, bleeding, starving, the wind howling like laughter through the trees.

And somewhere in the forest, a rabbit with a short tail and a split lip and yellow feet cupped a small, warm fire in his paws and grinned.

---

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. *Aknis de awtos mārtus.* Fire alone is the witness.

---

Lusifee survived. The Wild Cat always survives. But he never hunted the rabbit again. When asked, he would only say: *There was no rabbit. There was only an old man with long ears. There was only a village that vanished. There was only snow.* And the Otter, who had heard this kind of talk before, said nothing. And the Woodpeckers laughed. And the Bear, in his cave, opened one eye and closed it again.

Nokomis tended the fire. The rabbit came home. His tail was short. His lip was split. His feet were yellow. His eyes were bright.

"You conjured," she said.

"A little," he said.

"You are learning."

"A little."

"The Wild Cat will not forget."

"No," said the rabbit. "But he will not hunt me again. He hunted me three times. Three times he found what he was looking for. Three times it was not me."

"Who was it?"

"An old man with long ears," said the rabbit. "A chief with two daughters. A preacher with a mitre. I do not know. I have never met him."

Nokomis stirred the fire. The fire said nothing. The fire had seen everything. The fire did not need to speak.

# TALE V · THE CANNIBAL MONSTER

In the time after the Wild Cat hunt, the rabbit walked through the world with a short tail and a split lip and a fire in his paws and a new thing growing in his chest. The new thing was confidence — not the foolish confidence of the young rabbit who thought he could fish like Otter, but the earned confidence of a rabbit who had conjured villages from spruce twigs and preached sermons to a Wild Cat and vanished three times before morning. He was beginning to understand what Nokomis had meant when she named him *Fyrsthara*. The Fire-Carrier. The Lord who leaps. It was not a title. It was a job.

Nokomis watched him come and go. She was older now. She had been old before, but now she was very old. The fire kept her warm but could not keep her forever. She did not speak of this. She spoke of other things.

"You are helping," she said one evening.

"I am," said the rabbit.

"You are helping strangers."

"Yes."

"You are conjuring."

"A little."

"You are becoming known."

The rabbit looked at the fire. "Is that bad?"

"It is not bad," said Nokomis. "It is not good. It is what it is. The fire does not ask whether it should burn. It burns. You are the one who carries the fire. You will carry it to whoever needs it. That is what you are." She stirred the coals. "But the ones who need it will not always be grateful. And the ones who see you carrying it will not always be kind."

The rabbit thought about Lusifee, bleeding in the snow. "I know," he said.

"You do not know," said Nokomis. "But you will."

---

That winter, a boy came to the forest.

He was not a rabbit. He was not an animal at all. He was a young man from a village far to the east, and he was walking through the world looking for something he could not name. His parents had let him go because parents must let their children go, and because the boy had a restlessness in him that would have burned the village down if it had stayed. He walked for many days. He slept on the ground. He spoke to no one. He was not afraid of the forest. He was not afraid of anything yet. That would come later.

The rabbit found him at the edge of a clearing. The boy was sitting on a fallen log, eating a piece of dry bread. He looked up at the rabbit and did not seem surprised. In the old time, before the world was named, animals and humans spoke to each other as a matter of course. The boy had been walking long enough that the old time was starting to come back.

"Where are you going?" asked the rabbit.

"I do not know," said the boy.

"What are you seeking?"

"I do not know."

The rabbit sat down beside him. His short tail twitched. His split lip twitched. The fire in his paws was small and warm. "Then you are in the right place," he said. "The ones who know where they are going never find anything worth finding. The ones who do not know are the ones who stumble into the story."

They walked together. The rabbit did not plan to help the boy. The rabbit never planned to help anyone. Helping was not a decision. Helping was a reflex, like leaping, like conjuring, like the twitch of a tail that was no longer there.

---

They came to a village. The village was in mourning. The chief's daughter had been taken by a *kewahqu'* — a cannibal monster, a thing of teeth and hunger and endless appetite. It had come in the night. It had left tracks in the snow that were not the tracks of any animal the villagers knew. It had taken the girl and vanished into the northern forest. The chief had offered her hand in marriage to anyone who could bring her back. Many young men had gone. None had returned.

The boy listened to this story. His face was still. His hands were still. The restlessness that had driven him from his village was suddenly quiet.

"I will bring her back," he said.

The villagers looked at him. He was not tall. He was not strong. He carried no weapon. He had only a piece of dry bread and a rabbit with a short tail walking beside him.

The rabbit said nothing. He was remembering Nokomis's words. *You will carry the fire to whoever needs it. That is what you are.*

---

द्वारं धारय न तु द्वारं प्रयाहि
*Turan dare me turan pāi*
Hold the door — do not merely pass through it.

---

They went into the northern forest. The snow was deeper there. The trees were older. The rabbit walked beside the boy and the fire in his paws cast strange shadows on the drifts.

"The monster will kill you," said the rabbit.

"Maybe," said the boy.

"You have no weapon."

"No."

"You have no plan."

"No."

"Why are you going?"

The boy was quiet for a long time. They walked through the deep snow. The trees creaked around them. Somewhere far away, something howled.

"Because she is there," said the boy. "And I am here. And the distance between is the only thing I can change."

The rabbit looked at the boy. The boy looked ahead. The fire in the rabbit's paws grew a little brighter.

"You will do," said the rabbit.

---

They found the monster's lair by the smell. It was a cave in the side of a mountain, and the bones outside it were many, and the smell was the smell of things that had been eaten and not remembered. The girl was inside. They could hear her weeping.

The rabbit reached into the snow and picked up two squirrels. He smoothed them down with his paws. As he smoothed them, they grew. They grew until they were as large as the largest sled-dogs, with thick fur and bright eyes and breath that steamed in the cold air.

"Get on," said the rabbit.

The boy got on the squirrel-sledge. The rabbit got on behind him. The squirrels ran. They ran like the wind, like the fire, like the leap from the underworld. They crashed through the trees and the monster came out of its cave and saw them and roared. The roar shook the mountain. The boy did not flinch. The rabbit did not flinch. The squirrels ran faster.

The monster pursued them. It was vast and hairy and its teeth were many and its hunger was old. It had been hunting since before the world was named. It had never been outrun. The squirrels ran. The monster ran faster. The gap closed.

The rabbit reached into his paw — not the paw that held the fire, but the other paw, the one that was still learning — and pulled out a straw. A single blade of dry grass. He handed it to the boy.

"Eat this."

The boy ate it. He became a moose. A great bull moose with antlers like the branches of an ancient tree. He turned and faced the monster. The monster stopped. The monster had never faced a moose before. The monster had faced many things — hunters, warriors, fathers with axes and mothers with knives — but never a moose, never a boy who became a moose, never a rabbit who carried fire and conjured squirrels and pulled straws from his paw.

The boy-moose lowered his antlers. The monster hesitated. The rabbit whispered in the boy's ear: "Now."

The boy charged. The monster fell. The boy became a boy again, breathing hard, his hands shaking, his eyes bright.

---

यो मुञ्चति स मुच्यते
*Se leiket, se leiketai*
Whoever releases is released.

---

They brought the girl back to the village. The chief wept. The villagers cheered. The wedding feast was held, and the boy and the girl were married, and the rabbit danced with ornaments on his heels and his short tail bobbing. The girl was grateful. The boy was grateful. The rabbit was tired.

The young men of the village, who had not gone into the northern forest, who had not faced the monster, who had not become a moose, hated the boy. They hated him because he had done what they could not do. They hated him because he had returned when they had stayed. They hated him because the girl looked at him the way she had never looked at them.

They took him fishing on the sea. They raised a cry. "A whale is chasing us!" they shouted. "It is under the canoe!" And they knocked him overboard and paddled away like arrows in flight.

The boy floated in the cold water. The shore was far. The sky was gray. He thought of the girl. He thought of the squirrel-sledge. He thought of the rabbit. He did not think of the young men who had betrayed him. There was no room for them in his mind. There was only the cold and the sky and the distant shore.

A crow came. "Swim," said the crow. "I will bring aid." The crow flew away. The boy swam. The cold crept into his bones. The shore did not come closer.

The crow returned with a cord. It threw one end to the boy and towed him to a small island. "I can do no more," said the crow. "But there is another friend."

The boy sat on the island. He was starving. He was freezing. He was alone. A fox came. "Ha, friend," said the fox. "Are you here?" The fox picked a blade of dry grass from the ground and gave it to the boy. The boy ate it. He became a moose again. He ate the young grass until he was full. The fox gave him a second straw. He became a boy again.

"There is a village on the mainland," said the fox. "There is to be a great feast. A wedding feast." The fox's eyes were bright. "For the marriage of your wife to one of the men who pushed you overboard."

---

The boy crossed the water. He came to the village. He came to the feast. He came to his wife, who saw him and wept and kissed him and held him. He came to the young men who had betrayed him. They saw him. They were afraid.

He called to the fox. The fox came. The fox gave him straws. He changed the young men into a porcupine and a hog. They ran into the forest. They are there still. The boy and the girl lived together for many years. The rabbit watched from the edge of the firelight and then was gone.

---

Nokomis was waiting when he returned. She was very old now. The fire was low.

"You helped," she said.

"Yes."

"You conjured."

"A little."

"The boy lived."

"Yes."

"The girl lived."

"Yes."

"The monster is dead."

"Yes."

"The young men are a porcupine and a hog."

The rabbit's whiskers twitched. "That seemed fair."

Nokomis stirred the fire. "You are becoming what you are," she said. "The Fire-Carrier. The Lord who leaps. The one who helps strangers. The one who conjures. The one who turns enemies into what they always were." She looked at him with her clouded eyes. "It is almost time."

"Time for what?"

But Nokomis did not answer. The fire crackled. The snow fell. The rabbit sat in the warmth and did not know that the greatest trial was still to come, that the Tower was already rising on the plain of Shinar, that the builders were already writing their name on the cornerstone, that the scattering was already gathering like a storm beyond the edge of the world.

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. *Aknis de awtos mārtus.* Fire alone is the witness.

# TALE VI · THE LONG WINTER

The winter Nokomis died was the longest winter anyone could remember.

It began like any other. The snow came. The rabbit stored his carrots and his scavenged roots in the warren. The fire burned steady in the wigwam. Nokomis was old — she had been old since before the first winter, since before the first snow, since before the first fire was lit in the dark. The rabbit had stopped thinking about her age the way he had stopped thinking about the fire. Some things are simply there. You do not measure them. You do not imagine their absence. You build your life around their warmth.

But Nokomis knew. She had always known. She had known when the rabbit came home with a short tail and she had said nothing. She had known when the rabbit came home with a split lip and she had stirred the fire. She had known when the rabbit came home from the cannibal monster's lair with a boy who had been a moose and she had said: *It is almost time.*

She did not say what it was time for. She did not need to. The fire knew. The fire had been burning since before the world was worded, and it would burn until after the last word was spoken, and it had seen many grandmothers and many rabbits and many winters. The fire did not speak. The fire only burned. But the rabbit had learned to listen to what the fire did not say.

---

One morning Nokomis did not rise from her bed. The rabbit brought her tea. She drank it. Her hands shook more than usual. Her eyes were clouded but still sharp. She looked at the rabbit with the look she had given him when he came home from the Otter's lake, soaked and shivering and ashamed.

"You are afraid," she said.

"Yes," said the rabbit.

"That is good. Fear is not the enemy. Fear is the door. What you do with it — that is what matters."

"What do I do with it?"

"You carry it," she said. "The way you carry the fire. The way you carried the boy. The way you have carried everything since you stopped trying to be Otter and Woodpecker and Bear. You are the one who carries. That is what you are. That is what you have always been. That is what you will be when I am gone."

The rabbit's throat tightened. His short tail, which had not twitched in years, twitched. "You are not gone."

"Not yet," said Nokomis. "But soon. The fire will keep you warm. The fire will keep you company. The fire will remind you of me. But the fire cannot tell you stories. The fire cannot call you a fool when you are being foolish. The fire cannot make tea." She closed her eyes. "You will have to make your own tea."

---

She died that night. The fire burned low. The rabbit sat beside her. The snow fell outside the wigwam. The wind whispered through the cracks. The rabbit did not move for a long time.

He had carried fire from the underworld. He had carried a boy from a monster's lair. He had carried himself through the Wild Cat's hunt and the wedding feast and the pit where the old man had pulled his tail off. He had carried many things. He did not know how to carry this.

---

यो धारयति स धार्यते
*Se deret, se deretai*
Whoever holds is held.

---

The fire did not go out. The rabbit tended it. He did not know what else to do. He fed it twigs and bark and the dry moss Nokomis had gathered in the autumn. He sat by it through the long nights. He spoke to it. The fire did not answer. The fire had never answered. But the fire had always listened, and it listened now.

The winter stretched on. The snow grew deeper. The rabbit left the wigwam only to gather wood. He did not visit the Otter. He did not visit the Woodpeckers. He did not visit the Bear. He sat by the fire and remembered.

He remembered the Otter's slide and the cold water closing over his head. He remembered the Woodpeckers laughing and his head bleeding from the fishing-point. He remembered the Bear's knife and the cut on his own thin sole. He remembered the underworld — the dark, the creek, the glowing sprites, the fire waiting at the heart of everything. He remembered the old man in the pit and the tail that broke off short. He remembered the bride and the cord and the lip that split forever. He remembered Lusifee in the snow, bleeding, the old man with long ears vanishing three times before morning. He remembered the boy who became a moose and the girl who wept and the young men who became a porcupine and a hog.

He remembered Nokomis. Her voice. Her hands. Her tea. Her silence. The way she stirred the fire. The way she said his name.

---

One night, deep in the dark of the year, the rabbit went out into the snow. He walked to the place where the forest ended and the underworld began. The door was still there. It was closed. He did not try to open it. He sat beside it in the snow and let the cold press against his fur.

He did not leap. He did not conjure. He did not carry anything. He just sat.

The door remained closed. The snow fell. The rabbit's yellow feet grew white with cold. His long ears grew stiff with frost. He was not waiting for anything. He was not expecting anything. He was just there, at the edge of the underworld, where he had leaped so long ago, where the fire had recognized him, where he had become what he was.

After a long time, he stood up. He walked home. He fed the fire. He made himself tea.

---

The winter ended. The snow melted. The first green shoots pushed through the mud. The rabbit stood at the door of the wigwam and watched the forest come back to life. The fire burned behind him. His tail was short. His lip was split. His feet were yellow. His grandmother was gone.

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. The rabbit held the fire. The fire held the rabbit. Nokomis was gone, but the fire was still burning, and the tea was hot, and the forest was waking, and somewhere in the underworld the sprites were still telling the story of the rabbit who had come in the dark and left with the light.

The rabbit was alone. The rabbit was not alone. The rabbit was the first of the Metoullin.

# TALE VII · LOX


# TALE VII · THE WEAVING OF LOX

In the time after Nokomis died, the rabbit lived alone at the edge of the forest. The fire kept him warm. The memory of his grandmother kept him company. He made his own tea. It was not as good as hers, but it was hot, and it was his, and that was enough.

He was known now. The animals of the forest spoke of him in lowered voices. The Otter, when asked, would only say: *He was a poor fisherman, but he learned.* The Woodpeckers would laugh and say: *His head healed.* The Bear would shrug his great shoulders and say: *He tried. That is more than most.* The Wild Cat, when asked, would say nothing at all.

The rabbit walked the forest alone. His yellow feet left tracks in the snow that crossed and doubled back and led nowhere. He was not hiding. He was not running. He was simply walking. He had learned that walking is sometimes the only way to think, and thinking is sometimes the only way to grieve, and grieving is sometimes the only way to keep the fire burning when the fire is all you have.

One night the rabbit sat by the fire and felt the loneliness press against him like the cold against the walls of the wigwam. Nokomis was gone. The fire was warm, but the fire did not speak. The tea was hot, but the tea did not tell him he was a fool when he was being foolish. The rabbit was alone. He had been alone before — in the underworld, in the pit, in the Wild Cat's clearing — but this was a different kind of alone. This was the alone that settles in after grief, when the stories have all been told and the fire has burned low and there is nothing left to do but sit with yourself and see what rises.

What rose was a thought. The thought was this: *I could make someone.*

The rabbit sat with the thought for a long time. He did not leap at it. He had learned, in the long years since the Otter's slide, that some things need to be sat with. Some things need to be held in the mind the way the fire is held in the paws — carefully, attentively, without grasping.

*I could make someone,* he thought again. *A brother. Someone to carry the fire with me. Someone to talk to when the tea is too hot and the night is too long.*

He reached into the fire.

---

He did not reach with his paw. The fire would not burn him — the fire had never burned him — but the fire was not what he needed. He reached into the fire with the part of himself that had learned to carry things. The part that had cupped the first flame in the underworld. The part that had smoothed the squirrels into sled-dogs. The part that had pulled the straw from his palm and turned a boy into a moose. The part that Nokomis had recognized when she named him *Fyrsthara* — the Fire-Carrier, the Lord who leaps.

He reached into the fire and pulled out a thread of light.

It was thin. It was bright. It was the same light that had glowed in the creek in the underworld, the same light that had recognized him when he first cupped the flame. He held it in his paws and began to weave.

He wove all night. He wove the thread of light with spruce twigs and dry grass and the fur from his own chest. He wove with attention. He wove with memory. He wove with grief and love and the long loneliness of the winter. He wove the way Nokomis had woven baskets. He wove the way the Woodpeckers had woven their nests. He wove the way the fire wove warmth into the cold air of the wigwam.

He wove a brother.

---

When dawn came, a figure stood in the door of the wigwam. He had long ears and yellow feet and a short tail. He had a split lip. He looked exactly like the rabbit.

"Brother," said the figure.

The rabbit looked at him. His heart was full. His paws were sore. The fire had burned low. "Brother," he said.

The figure stepped into the light. His name was Lox. He was everything the rabbit was, made from twigs and grass and fur and light. He was warm. He was solid. He breathed.

And from the first moment, something was wrong.

---

It was small at first. Lox sat by the fire and drank the tea the rabbit made. He asked about Nokomis. He asked about the underworld. He asked about the Otter and the Woodpecker and the Bear. The rabbit told him everything. He had wanted someone to talk to. Now he had someone. He did not notice that Lox never told him anything in return.

It grew. Lox walked the forest while the rabbit slept. He met the Otter. "Your brother is a great fisherman," said the Otter later. "He told me so himself." The rabbit had told Lox about the Otter's slide. He had told Lox about the cold water closing over his head. Lox had heard what he wanted to hear.

He met the Woodpeckers. "Your brother is a great hunter," they said. "He told us of the rice he gathered from the highest trees." The rabbit had told Lox about the bleeding head and the laughter. Lox had heard what he wanted to hear.

He met the Bear. "Your brother is a great provider," said the Bear. "He told us he feeds his guests from his own flesh." The rabbit had told Lox about the knife and the cut and the failure. Lox had heard what he wanted to hear.

---

The rabbit sat by the fire. Lox sat across from him. The fire burned between them.

"You are telling stories," said the rabbit.

"I am telling stories," said Lox. "Is that not what we do?"

"You are telling stories that are not true."

Lox smiled. His split lip pulled wide. "They are true enough. The Otter believes them. The Woodpeckers believe them. The Bear believes them. What does it matter what happened, if what is believed is better?"

The rabbit looked at the fire. The fire looked at the rabbit. "It matters," said the rabbit. "The truth holds. The lie scatters."

"The truth," said Lox, "is that you failed at everything before you carried the fire. You were dragged from a lake. You were laughed at by woodpeckers. You cut your own foot while a bear watched. Why should I tell those stories? Why should I be the brother of a fool when I can be the brother of a hero?"

"Because I was the fool," said the rabbit. "The fool is who I was. The fool is how I learned. Without the fool, there is no fire."

Lox said nothing. The fire crackled. The snow fell outside the wigwam.

---

The rift grew. Lox began to want things. He wanted the fire for himself. He wanted the stories for himself. He wanted to be the one who carried, the one who leaped, the one who was known. But he did not want to earn it. He had been woven already complete — no failures, no losses, no long winter. He did not understand that the earning was the whole thing.

One day Lox came to the rabbit with a demand. "Teach me to make fire," he said. "The real fire. Not the conjuring. Not the tricks. The fire you carried from the underworld."

The rabbit looked at his brother. His heart was heavy. "I cannot teach you," he said. "The fire is not taught. The fire is carried. You must go down to the underworld yourself. You must follow the creek. You must let the sprites be shy. You must find the flame and let it recognize you."

"I do not want to go to the underworld," said Lox. "I want you to give me the fire."

"I cannot give it. It is not mine to give."

"You are selfish," said Lox. "You carry the fire and you will not share it. You are known and you will not let me be known. You are the Fire-Carrier and you will not let me carry."

The rabbit had no answer. The fire burned. The snow fell.

---

The Bear came to the wigwam the next day. The Bear was old now. His fur was gray around the muzzle. He moved slowly. But his eyes were still sharp.

"Your brother came to me," said the Bear. "He asked me to teach him to make fire."

The rabbit's ears flattened. "What did you tell him?"

"I told him I would teach him. He is your brother. He deserves a chance."

The Bear went to Lox. The rabbit watched from the door of the wigwam. The Bear gathered maple bark and made a little pile. "Watch," said the Bear. "This is how you make fire when you have no flint and steel." The Bear jumped over the pile. As soon as he jumped, the bark burst into flame.

"Now I give you the power," said the Bear. "Use it wisely."

Lox's eyes were bright. He did not thank the Bear. He did not ask how the power worked or what it cost or why the Bear had chosen to share it. He simply turned and ran.

---

He ran until he could no longer see the Bear. He gathered maple bark. He made a little pile. He jumped. The bark burst into flame. Lox laughed. He had never made fire before — not real fire, not the kind that comes from your own body, not the kind the Bear had carried since before the first winter.

He took out his flint and steel and threw them away. "These are no longer of any use," he said.

He walked on. After a dozen steps, he made another fire. Then another. Then another. He made fires every ten steps. He made fires for the joy of making them. He made fires to see the flame leap up. He made fires because he could. He did not need the fires. He did not use them to cook or to warm himself or to light the dark. He just made them, one after another, a trail of flames through the forest.

The power began to fade. He did not notice. He made another fire, and another, and another. The fires grew smaller. The flames grew weaker. The maple bark smoldered and went out.

By evening, the power was gone. Lox gathered a pile of bark and jumped. Nothing. He jumped again. Nothing. The forest was dark. The cold was settling in. He had thrown away his flint and steel. He had no way to make fire. He had used the gift for nothing, and now the gift was spent.

The cold grew. Lox jumped and jumped over piles of bark that would not ignite. His yellow feet grew white with frost. His long ears grew stiff. His short tail, which had never twitched with the memory of loss, twitched now. He kept jumping. The cold grew worse. He froze to death while he was jumping, his body suspended in the act of trying, a sculpture of hunger and waste.

---

Spring came. The snow melted. Lox lay where he had fallen, frozen and still. His younger brother — not the rabbit, but Raccoon, who had been made later and differently and with less attention — came walking through the forest and found him.

Raccoon went over and tried to wake him. "Older brother," he said, "get up. You are oversleeping. It is very late."

Lox rubbed his eyes. He sat up. He was stiff and cold and hollow, but he was alive. "Younger brother," he said, "I overslept. I would have lain there forever if you had not come by and awakened me."

He got his strength back. He was as strong as ever. But he had learned nothing. The hollow cannot learn. The hollow can only thaw.

---

The rabbit heard of this. He sat by the fire and said nothing for a long time. The fire burned. The tea was hot.

"He is my brother," the rabbit said finally. "I wove him without grief. I wove him without failure. I wanted him to be spared what I suffered. And so he learned nothing. And so he froze."

The fire did not answer. The fire had seen many brothers and many failures and many long winters. The fire only burned.

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. The rabbit had held the fire. Lox had held the fire and thrown it away. The rabbit had learned through failure. Lox had been spared failure and learned nothing. The fire still burned. The tea was still hot. The brother was still hollow.

# TALE VIII · THE WHALE

In the time after Lox walked into the forest, the rabbit traveled. He had never traveled before — not really. He had gone down to the underworld and come back. He had gone to the northern forest and faced the cannibal monster. He had gone to the meadow and watched the fox. But he had never simply walked to see what was there. Nokomis was gone. Lox was gone. The wigwam was empty. The fire was warm but the walls were quiet. The rabbit needed to move.

He walked east. He walked until the forest thinned and the ground grew rocky and the air began to taste of salt. He had never tasted salt before. He had never seen the sea. He stood on a cliff and looked out at the gray water and thought: *The world is larger than I knew.*

A village stood at the edge of the sea. The rabbit approached it. The village was quiet. Too quiet. No smoke rose from the cooking fires. No children ran between the lodges. No fishermen mended their nets on the shore. The rabbit's ears flattened. He knew this kind of quiet. It was the quiet of the *kewahqu'*. It was the quiet of something that had come and taken and not returned what it took.

An old woman sat alone by a cold fire pit. Her face was lined. Her hands were empty. The rabbit sat down beside her.

"Grandmother," he said, "where are your people?"

She looked at him with eyes that had seen too much. "The Whale took them," she said. "It lies in the bay. It opens its mouth and the water rushes in. Anything near is drawn down. Our fishermen. Our young men. My grandson." She gestured at the empty village. "We were many. Now we are few. Soon we will be none."

"Where is the Whale now?"

"Where it always is. In the bay. Waiting."

The rabbit looked at the bay. The water was gray and still. In the center, a dark shape lay just below the surface. It was vast. It was patient. It was hungry.

---

The rabbit walked down to the shore. He stood at the edge of the water and looked at the dark shape. He was not afraid. He had been afraid before — in the underworld, in the pit, in the Wild Cat's clearing. Fear was an old friend now. Fear was the door. What you did with it — that was what mattered.

He reached into his fur. Not the fire — the fire was back at the wigwam, burning low, waiting for him. He reached into his fur and found what he always found when he needed it: a small piece of flint. He did not know where it had come from. Perhaps Nokomis had put it there. Perhaps it had always been there. Perhaps the fire had given it to him without him noticing.

He waded into the water. The cold bit at his yellow feet. He waded deeper. The dark shape did not move. It was waiting. It knew he was coming. The rabbit began to sing.

*You, who open your mouth,*
*Great One, you draw them in.*
*So I have heard it told.*
*Gather me in.*
*Gather me in.*

---

The Whale opened its mouth.

The water rushed forward. The rabbit was swept off his feet. He tumbled through the cold and the foam and the dark, and then he was inside.

It was dark. Not the dark of the underworld — the dark of something alive. The walls were soft and warm and moved with a slow, terrible rhythm. The air was thick and foul. And there were people there.

They huddled in the dark. Some were alive. Some were dead. Some were dying. They had no fire. They had no food. They had been swallowed days ago, weeks ago, and the Whale did not chew and the Whale did not digest and the Whale simply held them there, in the warm dark, waiting.

The rabbit found a young woman among the living. Her eyes were dull. Her hands were limp. "Granddaughter," he said, "look in my fur. Find the flint."

She stirred. Her hands moved through his fur — the fur of his chest, where he had pulled the thread of light to weave his brother. She found the flint. A small piece. A nothing piece. A piece you would throw away if you did not know what it was.

"Give it to me," said the rabbit.

He struck the flint against his paw. "Grow bigger," he said. The flint grew. He struck it again. "Grow bigger." It grew again. Four times he struck it, and each time it grew. Then he struck it a fifth time and said, "Be a knife." And it became a knife. A great knife. A knife of flint and fire and the attention of a rabbit who had learned to conjure from grief.

---

He felt along the ribs of the Whale. The ribs were vast, curving up into the dark like the beams of a great hall. He found a soft place between two bones. He cut. The knife bit deep. He cut a door.

"Go," he said to the people. "Go through the door. Swim for the shore. The Whale will not follow. The Whale will be dead."

They went. One by one, the living carried the dying, and the dying carried the dead, and all of them passed through the door the rabbit had cut between the ribs of the Whale. The rabbit waited until the last one was gone. Then he ran forward, into the dark, toward the heart.

The heart was vast. It beat with a slow, heavy rhythm that shook the walls. The rabbit stood before it. He was small. He was very small. He had always been small.

He drove the knife into the heart.

---

The Whale thrashed. The sea boiled. The rabbit was thrown from side to side, tumbling through the dark, and then a rush of water carried him up and out and into the light. He floated on the surface of the bay. The Whale floated beside him, vast and still. The people were on the shore. They were waving. They were crying. They were alive.

The rabbit swam to shore. His fur was soaked. His yellow feet were cut from the flint. His ears rang with the memory of the heartbeat. He lay on the sand and breathed.

---

The old woman came to him. She looked at him with her lined face and her empty hands and her eyes that had seen too much.

"You went inside," she said.

"Yes."

"You cut the door."

"Yes."

"You killed the Whale."

"Yes."

She was quiet for a moment. "My grandson was already dead," she said. "He was dead when the Whale swallowed him. But the others — the others are alive. Because of you."

The rabbit sat up. His fur was drying in the salt wind. His paws were sore. His heart was full. "I am sorry about your grandson," he said.

"I am sorry about your grandmother," she said.

The rabbit looked at her. He had not told her about Nokomis. He had not told her anything.

"How did you know?"

The old woman smiled. Her face was still lined. Her hands were still empty. But her eyes were different now. "You carry the fire," she said. "Only those who have lost someone can carry the fire. That is the first rule. The fire does not go to the happy. The fire goes to the ones who have sat in the dark and learned to make their own light."

The rabbit looked at his paws. They were cut and sore. They were the paws of a rabbit who had failed at fishing and failed at woodpeckering and failed at being a bear. They were the paws of a rabbit who had carried fire from the underworld and a boy from a monster's lair and a village from the belly of a whale. They were the paws of a rabbit whose grandmother had died and who had learned to make his own tea.

"Yes," he said. "That is the first rule."

---

He stayed in the village for three days. The people fed him. The people thanked him. The people asked him to stay forever. But the rabbit was not made for staying. He was made for leaping. He was made for carrying. He was made for walking into the forest and seeing what was there.

On the fourth day, he left. The old woman stood on the shore and watched him go. The Whale lay in the bay, vast and still, and the gulls were already nesting on its back. The rabbit walked west. His fur was dry. His paws were healing. The fire was waiting. The tea would be hot.

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held. The rabbit had held the people. The people had held the rabbit. The Whale had held them all, and now the Whale was dead, and the gulls were nesting, and the sea was gray and still.

---

In the time after the Whale, the rabbit walked home.

The forest was green. The snow had melted. The path was the same path he had always walked — past the Otter's lodge, past the Woodpeckers' tree, past the Bear's cave. The Otter was fishing. He waved. The Woodpeckers were tapping. They nodded. The Bear was sleeping. He snored.

The wigwam was still there. The fire was still burning. The tea was cold, but the pot was full. The rabbit sat down by the fire and warmed his yellow feet and thought about all the things that had happened since he first leaped into the underworld with nothing but failure and hunger and a grandmother who believed in him.

He had carried fire from the dark. He had lost his tail helping an old man out of a pit. He had split his lip twisting a cord too fast. He had conjured villages from spruce twigs and preached sermons to a Wild Cat and vanished three times before morning. He had turned a boy into a moose and enemies into porcupines and hogs. He had buried his grandmother and woven a brother and watched that brother squander the gift and freeze. He had been swallowed by a Whale and cut his way out and freed the people and split the heart.

He was the same rabbit. He was not the same rabbit. His tail was short. His lip was split. His feet were yellow. His fur was gray around the muzzle now. He was getting old. Not as old as Nokomis — no one was as old as Nokomis — but old enough to know that the stories would outlast him.

He stirred the fire. The fire said nothing. The fire had seen everything. The fire would see everything. The fire was the first thing and the last thing and the thing that held the door between them.

---

He was the Fire-Carrier. He was the Lord who leaps. He was the one who carries. He was the one who fails and gets up. He was the one whose tail is short and whose lip is split and whose feet are yellow. He was Mātaṅgvās. He was Mateguas. He was Mategwas. He was the Rabbit. He was the story.

---

*Se deret, se deretai.* Whoever holds is held.

*Aknis de awtos mārtus.* Fire alone is the witness.

*Dabos de awtos mārtus.* The Rabbit alone is the witness.

*Asiyan de awtos mārtus.* Truth alone is the witness.

---

The fire holds. The tea is hot. The Rabbit runs still.
